Heritage and Belonging in the Danube Region

Ethnographic perspectives on cultural continuity and displacement

About the Project

This project examines how communities along the Danube construct and contest heritage narratives in the context of political transformation and cross-border mobility. Drawing on eighteen months of ethnographic fieldwork across Hungary and Romania, it traces the everyday practices through which belonging is negotiated at the intersection of kinship, place, and institutional governance.

A central concern is how heritage regimes — both state-led and grassroots — mediate relationships between displaced populations and the landscapes they claim as home. The research contributes to anthropological debates on the politics of memory, material culture, and the affective dimensions of placemaking.

The project combines participant observation, life-history interviews, and archival research to build a multi-layered account of how cultural continuity is maintained, improvised, and sometimes deliberately ruptured across generations.
